Optimizing Your Resume

When we work at small companies, what is our advantage? More time! (If you’re doing 996, forget I said anything) The fastest way for an interviewer to get to know you is through your resume, so don’t slack off on this step!

Keep these points in mind:

  1. Don’t put anything on your resume that you’re not familiar with
  2. Use technical keywords to describe your projects (The opening of every interview is “Please introduce yourself” — preparing this in advance will make your performance much more relaxed and natural)
  3. Either find your resume template on github or build one yourself. If you can’t even build a resume template, then buddy, you’ve got more work to do! (Can’t even write a resume template and you want to get into big tech???)

If unfamiliar topics get asked in depth and you can’t answer, it’ll hurt your impression score and affect your confidence. Using technical keywords not only lets the interviewer quickly understand your project's technical highlights, but also greatly influences the interviewer’s question direction!!!! This is extremely important. You can check my interview notes — the repetition rate of questions is very high, precisely because my resume had already guided the interviewer toward the knowledge areas I had prepared! If you feel your project experience isn’t impressive and lacks technical highlights, I think it’s because you don’t understand your project well enough, or you don’t know how to sell it.

Let me give you an example:

  • Suppose your project only involved making API requests. Couldn’t you write about implementing a concurrent request queue using Promises? (Don’t tell me you can’t — if you can’t, can’t you learn??)
  • Suppose your project only used a mainstream framework to build some pages. Couldn’t you write about mastering browser compatibility solutions and being familiar with page rendering optimization? (Can’t? Can’t you learn?)
  • If you really can’t think of anything, just leave a comment below and ask me. We’ll all help you brainstorm!! (Who doesn’t know how to talk big? But you also need to back up what you claim, so it’s actually a great learning process)

Creating a resume doesn’t just help interviewers quickly understand you — it also helps you better understand yourself. Know your strengths and weaknesses!

Suggestions:

  • First, create your ideal resume (with knowledge points including both what you’re familiar with and what you’re less familiar with but want to include)
  • Then, based on your resume, master each knowledge point
  • Before submitting, remove the ones you’re not confident in

This way, you’ll have a learning process, step by step working toward your ideal resume!

Leetcode / ACwing Strategy

Although front-end work rarely involves algorithms in practice, learning algorithmic thinking will definitely benefit you greatly! I recommend studying by topic, making targeted breakthroughs. Set a plan for yourself. Here’s my recommended order (adjust based on your situation): Learn bit operations early — they come in handy for many problems.

  • x >> 1 === Math.floor(x / 2)
  • x & 1 === 0 even number
  • x & 1 === 1 odd number
  • x & x === x
  • x ^ x === 0
  1. Sorting (must know quicksort, should know mergesort, divide-and-conquer thinking)
  2. Binary search
  3. Strings
  4. Maps / Arrays
  5. Linked lists / Two pointers
  6. Recursion / Stacks / Queues
  7. Trees / Binary search trees / Balanced binary trees
  8. DFS / BFS
  9. Backtracking
  10. Dynamic programming
  11. Greedy algorithms
  12. Graphs …

Accumulating algorithm knowledge takes the most time (depends on aptitude — mine isn’t that high, took me half a year), but it brings many rewards. It’s not just about solving problems — more importantly, it’s about learning the thinking. For example, React's time slicing uses the divide and conquer approach. Knowledge is truly interconnected!

Accumulating Interview Experience

Recommended process:

  1. Start with smaller companies to practice your self-introduction and fluency with resume knowledge points
  2. After every interview, record the questions and fill in the gaps
  3. When smaller companies can no longer stump you, start interviewing at big companies
  4. Repeat step 2 until big companies can’t stump you either. By then, you should have collected some offers
  5. Practice negotiation skills. With offers in hand, you can practice bargaining with HR. Their job is to push down your price, so don’t show weakness. Start with a high number and negotiate from there, using your ideal salary as the bottom line. If you performed well during the interview, they’ll most likely accept. And if they manage to lower the price a bit, they’ll feel like they succeeded too, while you still get what you wanted
  6. With all this, you’ll be more confident and have leverage. Yes, remember — you are an emotionless interview machine! Go apply to your dream target!
